Welcome to Creative Early Childhood Education

Shaping tomorrow's innovators today

Creativity is one of the important principles of early childhood education and care for children’s development of cognitive, social, and emotional. In a rapidly emerging knowledge base, creativity helps children discover concepts they are interested in, give vent to their emotions or feelings and experience and test their environment (Berk, 2018). This website aims at availing resource and implementation technique to teachers in tackling creativity in curriculum and subject areas in early childhood learning.

Why Creativity Matters:

Cognitive Development: Creativity call for improvement of evoked cognitive abilities including of problem solving and critical thinking. For imagination is the best tool that, during the play, enhances children’s thinking abilities to deal with abstract concepts and analyze the complexity of their problems (Gopnik, 2017). Such activities foster creativity and cognitive flexibility that is the ability of a child to change the mode of operation of his or her brain and adapt well in circumstances (Berk, 2018).

Emotional Expression:

Creativity on the part of children is helpful since it provides an avenue of expressing feelings. To this effect, artistic and dramatic activities serve as an enabling tool for children to express their emotional status hence leading to the preschooler’s regulation of the feelings, especially frustration (Berk, 2018). The creative activity would also help a child to make sense of things or feelings within expressive-self as children would discover the inner aspect of them

Social Skills:

Creativity related tasks have social relationships and team working embedded within them. Collaborative projects and group projects force the participants to have to communicate and work together as well as understand the feelings of others. In frolic play, the children get to be in a position to arrange as well as coordinate, share knowledge and ideas as well as encourage team members, which are pivotal skills for beneficial social relations (Vygotsky, 1978).

Art

Introduction to Art in Early Childhood Education

It was seen that art education plays a vital role in the education of early childhood children especially in developing their creativity. It offers children with chance to come up with their creations and to explore the affective domain through media. Arts education boosts the use of the hands and brains, problem-solving skills, and over-all learning (Eisner 2002; Gallagher 2019).

Materials:

Some of the requirements that help make art include paint, brushes, clay, markers as well as different types of papers. It is also possible to use fabric or sand paper or even aluminum foil to help a child see different textures in making pieces of art (Koster, 2009). Digital Tools: Drawing Applications and software in the digital environment gives children another platform through which to explore artmaking with fresh media that can enrich the possibilities offered by materials that would traditionally be used (Gallas, 1994).

Pedagogical Practices:

Open-Ended Projects: Make the alternatives for children to be able to do more open-ended art projects that would foster a creative mind and free thinking. Such project enable children to have voice and make decisions regarding their projects hence enhancing freedom and creativity (Vygotsky, 1978). Thematic Exploration: Engage children in learning through art by choosing certain topics as seasons, cultures or kinds of emotions. This approach assists children to establish relationships between the concepts created and the goals of learning hence improving on their knowledge of different areas (Eisner, 2002).

Learning Experiences:

0-2 Years: Sensory painting activities inclusive of edible colors and textured objects helps the infants in touch and vision senses. This activity is beneficial to the sensory development as well as the early art experience for the child (Fisher, 2011). 2-3 Years: Manipulating collages with different textures of pieces of material, you can improve the use of fingers in babies, and invention. As the child combines the different materials, the child gets to know their area of interest in arts while at the same time improving their spacial awareness (Hargreaves, 2009). 3-5 Years: Solving a task by creating animals or landscapes with them out of various elements stimulates the child’s imagination and helps the child to learn problem-solving skills. These topics can easily be fit into other learning areas so that art links with different areas of study (Golub, 2004). 6-8 Years: In turn, such advanced forms of art as printmaking facilitate children deeper engagement in artistic practices. Printmaking activities, as one of the arts teaching strategies, enable children to appreciate notions such as texture, pattern and composition (Bresler, 2006).

Drama and Puppetry

Social drama for health promotion in schools

The dramatic and puppetry activities should be used for the development of speech, social and emotional skills of the child. The activities facilitate access to the diverse viewpoints and also the emotions are depicted by feelings through role play and storytelling (O’Neill, 1995; Boal, 2002).

Materials:

The dramatic play requires the use of puppets, costumes, and props in order to make the learning fun. These assist children to role-play and get to experience sampling different characters and events (Johnson & O’Neill, 1984).

Digital Tools:

Besides, there are applications which allow children to perform puppetry and use other online resources related to drama. These sorts of tools may extend more conventional forms of puppet workshops and performances and open up new possibilities for play (Heathcote & Bolton, 1995).

Learning Experiences:

0-2 Years: Sensory painting activities inclusive of edible colors and textured objects helps the infants in touch and vision senses. This activity is beneficial to the sensory development as well as the early art experience for the child (Fisher, 2011). 2-3 Years: Manipulating collages with different textures of pieces of material, you can improve the use of fingers in babies, and invention. As the child combines the different materials, the child gets to know their area of interest in arts while at the same time improving their spacial awareness (Hargreaves, 2009). 3-5 Years: Solving a task by creating animals or landscapes with them out of various elements stimulates the child’s imagination and helps the child to learn problem-solving skills. These topics can easily be fit into other learning areas so that art links with different areas of study (Golub, 2004). 6-8 Years: In turn, such advanced forms of art as printmaking facilitate children deeper engagement in artistic practices. Printmaking activities, as one of the arts teaching strategies, enable children to appreciate notions such as texture, pattern and composition (Bresler, 2006). 0-2 Years: Infants are familiarized with rudimentary ideas of dramatic play and social communication by basic puppet show for babies. Some of them include: The use of soft textured puppets, which can enable the caregivers to engage the infants in storytelling as well as educating the infants about the various textures when taken through exploration (Newman & Simoes, 2017). 2-3 Years: Performing basic storytelling that uses content appropriate to children and involves the use of different forms of prop and puppets makes toddlers to be better in language and social development. It makes the childhood development more interactive mostly because it entails a lot of acting and storytelling (Woodson 2014). 3-5 Years: Engaging in early learners in the production of the mannered play scenarios and performance helps the learners gain confidence and improve on collaboration skills. All of these activities help children to develop such skills as roleplay, decision making and emotional regulation (Keller, 2018). 6-8 Years: Dramatization helps the child, especially the elder, to be in a position to understand and act on presented narrative and themes on a play. This activity hone the creative skills, group working and presentation skills of the students, besides acquainting them with the aspect of dramatic narration (Thompson 2009).
