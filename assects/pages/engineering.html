Introduction to Engineering in Early Childhood Education

In engineering curriculum in early childhood education it is emphasized on problem solving, creativity as well as scientific skills. In engineering activities, children construct, design and critically evaluate solutions to existing practical challenges, as pointed by Berk (2018). Professional engineering helps in developing reasoning ability and understanding the principle behind every thing.

Materials:

Blocks, construction kits, gears and other construction like shapes and blocks are preferred in engineering activities. Other equipments include simple hand tools and materials such as carboard and plastics can also be employed in coming up with prototypes as well as testing of the designs (Cunningham & Carlsen, 2014). Digital Tools: Some of the themes include, Engineering Apps and software that give children a rich way to learn engineering. These tools can enhance other practical tasks and can provide students with other perspective to learn engineering ideas (Verenikina, 2008).

Design Thinking:

Engage children in design thinking stages, to encourage them solve problems, generate ideas and come up with prototypes. The extent of this method is that it fosters creative approaches to problem-solving and encourages the process of learning by trial and error. Project-Based Learning: Include a number of project based learning activity that would enable the children to work on engineers for projects for a longer time. This approach promotes collaboration, creativity as well as incorporation of the engineering skills (Krajcik & Blumenfeld, 2006).

Learning Experiences:

0-2 Years: Playing with large blocks or stacking cups it is also advisable for infants as they help them develop skills of understanding spatial relations and basic principles of engineering. These activities promote problem solving and/ or creative thinking (Johnson, Christie & Wardle, 2005). 2-3 Years: Building with blocks or making tunnels with tubes are some sample ways that various materials can be used to support the constructive learning of toddlers because these activities also foster motor skills and spatial sense. These activities support discovery and testing (Ginsburg, 2007). 3-5 Years: Constructing works as constructing a car or construction of spaces for learning improves the engineering education of the children. By engaging children in a process where they plan, build something and assess it thus enables them to develop their problem solving skills (Berk, 2018). 6-8 Years: If the engineering challenges are more complicated like creating a simple tool or working on an engineering challenge, then children of a more elevated age can use science systematically. Such activities facilitate development of creativity, teamwork and technical competencies of students.
