Mathematics and Numeracy

Introduction to Mathematics and Numeracy

Counting, number sense solving skills and reasoning are components in early childhood to build strongfoundation to be applied in mathematics. Teaching young children concepts in mathematics involves using teaching aids and materials that they use to manipulate in their play during the early ages of their learning. Point, level, shape, size, and pattern are example areas of learning that is boosted by child’s mathematical play according to Copley (2000).

Materials:

Counting tools including beads, counters and number cards together with shape and pattern blocks help in the development of early mathematics. Some of the benefits of games and manipulatives are that it enables the children to make a concrete representation of the abstract thought in mathematics (Copley, 2000).

Digital Tools:

Maths applications and educational software offer child friendly approaches of handling concerns with mathematics. These tools can also help with delivering content tailored to individual learners, and reiterate concepts as well as facts through quizzes and games and other type of activities (Miller & Almon, 2009).

Learning Experiences:

0-2 Years: Tactile for example, when using objects where the infant has to sort the objects by colors and size will enhance the ability to count. Games such as counting and shape recognition help in the cognitive and spatial areas as mentioned by Bredekamp & Copple (2009). 2-3 Years: Various manipulative items such as blocks that can be stacked as well as study of patterns helps the toddler to develop numeral knowledge. Grouping of objects based on the various attributes promotes early problem-solving, as well as classification abilities (Ginsburg, 2009). 3-5 Years: Learning games and puzzles that include counting, addition, and or subtraction are helpful for early childhood education center when imparting scholars with simple number sense. Such activities enhance mathematical learning and reasoning and learning through various playful learning processes (NRC, 2009). 6-8 Years: simple and complex operational activities such as estimating the measurements to be used in cooking or simple arithmetic word problems help in development of concepts of measurement, addition, subtraction and problem solving in older children (Sarama & Clements 2009). Pedagogical Practices: Hands-On Learning: Include movements which enable children to perform physical activities on mathematics in order to make use of their hands. Such an approach aids in comprehension of the real life issues since; Children can relate to the real life experiences when grasping abstract concepts (Copley, 2000). Integrated Activities: teach students to apply mathematics problems in different content areas, for instance, making Mathematics connections to Science or Art. For instance, while cooking, one can use the measuring cups to measure the ingredients in a recipe and this can integrate mathematics with seminal patterns. (NIC, 2009)
