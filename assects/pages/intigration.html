Integrated Curriculum (STEM, STEAM, STREAM and STEMIE)

Introduction to Integrated Curriculum

STEM (Science, Technology, Engineering, Mathematics), STEAM (with Arts included), STREAM (with Reading included) and STEMIE (with Inclusion and Ethics incorporated) is an interconnected teaching learning model which interlinks different subjects. This approach helps with acquiring all facets of skill and incorporates the actual issues (Beers, 2011; Ponder, 2017).

Materials:

Science kits, engineering manipulatives and art materials together with technology tools as shown in the following picture form the tegular column of resources to support integrated learning activities. These resources facilitate children in making links from one subject with other subjects (Horizon Research, 2015). Digital Tools: Mobile applications and software regarding to more than one topic of education could improve integrated learning. These tools make it possible for children to engage in connection between the different areas of knowledge in an interactivity form (Robinson, 2011).

Pedagogical Practices:

Project-Based Learning: Organise learning activities in form of projects to assimilate cross-disciplinary learning experiences into a single project. Through this method, the children are able to use knowledge from different areas in order to solve a particular problem and also improve on comprehensive skills (Krajcik & Blumenfeld, 2006). Cross-Disciplinary Connections: Make connections between and across different disciplines by establishing practical relations between the points derived from science, technology, engineering, art and mathematics. It assists children to comprehend how what they are learning can be of value as well as how it can be used (Ponder, 2017).

Learning Experiences:

0-2 Years: Play at the integrated level with the help of toys combined with sensory investigation and use of simple technical items assists the infant in the ability underlying relations between different categories of experiences. For instances, moving toys on a water table is also a good way of teaching essentials of science and Mathematics (Fleer, 2011). 2-3 Years: Play that involves art and science includes preparing food colouring simple experiments and mixing items; such aspects help the toddlers to develop appreciations regarding relationships between different fields of study. Such activities promote creativity and scientific thinking (Gopnik, 2017). 3-5 Years: Raising the state of knowledge, themed projects, in which information from one subject is combined with information from another area of knowledge, such as the creation of a model of a community with the use of painting, art and computations in the mathematics classroom are necessary to explain to children how everything is interconnected. In these projects there is modelling of comprehensive and systematic approaches to learning as well as problem solving as noted by the National Research Council 2012. 6-8 Years: Multi-faceted projects, which involve STEM, STEAM, STREAM, or STEMIE aspects, like developing a sustainable community or investigating an environmental concern for children, help the older children solve complex academic challenges in the real world. Such projects foster a critical value dimension and teamwork skills in the context of the academy (Beers, 2011).
